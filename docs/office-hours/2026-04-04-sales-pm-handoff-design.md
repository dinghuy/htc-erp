## Problem Statement

HTC ERP should not try to be a generic cross-functional suite first. The first thing it must do is stop won deals from stalling between commercial approval and execution ownership.

The sharp product problem is:

`A sales-facing deal becomes "won" or commercially ready, but the next operational steps do not become cleanly owned, approved, and visible fast enough.`

That creates delay, confusion, rework, and blame across sales, project management, procurement, finance, and leadership.

## Demand Evidence

Current demand evidence is internal and workflow-based, not market-facing yet.

What we know from this session:

- the target wedge is `Sales-PM handoff`, not “all departments”
- the current status quo is `Excel + Chat + Memory`
- the root pain you picked is `Stop Handoff Delays`
- the non-obvious observation you picked is `Approval Is The Real Bottleneck`
- the reason to build now is that `Complexity Has Outgrown Sheets`

This is enough to justify a focused internal product wedge.

It is not yet enough to justify a huge platform build. The roadmap should assume the first proof point is reducing handoff delay and approval ambiguity on real deals, not broad adoption claims.

## Status Quo

Today the handoff lives across:

- spreadsheets tracking commercial and execution details
- chat threads carrying context and exceptions
- people remembering whether the last approval, pricing assumption, or next owner is “already agreed”

That means the “truth” of a won deal is fragmented. The system of record is not actually a system.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

### Target user

The first user is the person bridging commercial win into project execution:

- a sales user who also behaves like a project coordinator
- or a combined Sales-PM user who inherits the handoff mess from both sides

### Narrowest wedge

The narrowest version worth building is:

`Won Quote -> Clean Handoff`

That means one flow where:

- the quote is approved or won
- the next owner is explicit
- required approvals are visible
- the next task/project/sales-order step is unambiguous
- the handoff does not disappear into spreadsheet rows and chat threads

## Constraints

- keep the current stack, Preact/Vite frontend and Express/TypeScript backend
- keep the roadmap anchored to the revenue flow, not generic collaboration
- avoid broad “all departments at once” launches
- use the current repo maturity, several pieces already exist in partial form
- require deterministic verification for workflow and permission changes

## Premises

### Premise 1

`Approval is the real bottleneck.`

Probably true. Strong premise.

Why it matters:
- if no one knows whether they are allowed to move, work stalls even when the data is present
- this turns the product into a confidence system, not just a record system

Risk:
- “approval” can become a catch-all explanation for every kind of friction
- some delays may actually come from bad ownership, missing data, or poor route clarity

What to verify:
- on recent won deals, measure whether the delay came from waiting for sign-off, unclear owner, or missing project data

### Premise 2

`Stop handoff delays` is a better first promise than reporting or dashboard polish.

I agree.

Why it matters:
- a buyer pays faster to stop dropped work than to get nicer visibility
- reporting becomes trustworthy only after the handoff mechanics are trustworthy

Risk:
- if the team over-builds infrastructure around this promise, the wedge gets diluted into “workflow platform”

### Premise 3

`One project workspace` should be the center of the product.

Likely right.

Why it matters:
- pricing, approvals, tasks, procurement, finance, and delivery all need a shared object
- the handoff moment is where fragmented modules do the most damage

Risk:
- workspace sprawl, too many tabs, too much unfinished depth
- if every team gets a tab before the core handoff flow is sharp, the workspace becomes a shell around unresolved ambiguity

### Premise 4

`Cross-functional depth` should expand after the handoff wedge works.

Strong and important.

Why it matters:
- this preserves coherence
- it prevents the roadmap from becoming “ERP-lite plus dashboards plus chat”

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: Full Cross-Functional Workspace From Day One

Build the first version as a broad role-based system for sales, PM, procurement, accounting, legal, and director personas with deep workspace coverage across all tabs.

Pros:
- ambitious
- looks complete on paper
- aligns with the long-term vision

Cons:
- too much surface area before the wedge is proven
- likely to create fake progress, many tabs, thin workflows
- makes it easy to polish reports and shell UX while the core handoff still leaks

Verdict:
tempting, but not a good first move

### Approach B: Handoff Control System Centered On Won Quote -> Clean Handoff

Build a product that is explicitly about the transition from approved/won quotation into owned execution.

The first promise is:

`We stop won deals from stalling because nobody owns the next approval, task, or blocker.`

Pros:
- narrow enough to ship
- painful enough to matter
- creates the correct center of gravity for later workspace expansion
- gives the team a measurable before/after outcome

Cons:
- less glamorous than a “full platform” pitch
- requires discipline to defer adjacent ideas

Verdict:
this is the right starting point

## Recommended Approach

Choose Approach B.

The product should behave like a handoff control system first, then earn the right to become a deeper cross-functional workspace.

That means the first roadmap slice should optimize for four truths:

1. a commercially ready deal has a clean next owner
2. required approvals are explicit, not implied
3. the next operational step is visible and allowed
4. the project workspace becomes the place where this truth lives

Everything else should be judged against that.

If a proposed feature does not make the handoff cleaner, faster, or safer, it belongs later.

## Open Questions

- Which recent handoff failures were caused by approval ambiguity versus ownership ambiguity versus missing data?
- Who actually feels the pain first, the sales user, the project manager, or the combined Sales-PM operator?
- What is the first measurable success metric, time-to-owned-handoff, time-to-sales-order-release, or percentage of won deals with a clean next step inside the system?
- Does `admin` really belong in the executive-home family long-term, or is that just a temporary composition convenience?

## Success Criteria

- a won quote can be converted into a clean operational handoff without spreadsheet-only side channels
- the next owner, next approval, and next action are obvious in one screen
- stalled handoffs become visible before the customer feels the delay
- leadership reporting improves as a consequence of cleaner workflow truth, not as the primary product promise
- the product can expand into procurement/finance/legal around the same project record without losing coherence

## Dependencies

- canonical role, permission, workflow, and audit contracts
- shared project workspace shell
- server-side workflow transition enforcement
- deterministic QA and role/route regression coverage

## The Assignment

Do one real-world validation pass before expanding the roadmap further:

Take the last 5 won deals and write down, for each one:

- when commercial approval finished
- who owned the next step
- what the next step actually was
- where the team had to leave the system and use sheet/chat/memory
- what delayed the handoff most

If 3 out of 5 failures are really approval/ownership ambiguity, the wedge is confirmed. If not, tighten the problem before building more.
